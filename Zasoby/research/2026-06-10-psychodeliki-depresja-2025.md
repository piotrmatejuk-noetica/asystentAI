# Psychedelics and Ketamine for Depression: Clinical Trial Evidence from 2024–2025

Across 2024 and 2025, clinical research on psychedelic-assisted therapies and ketamine-based interventions for depression has moved from small, proof‑of‑concept studies into large, late‑phase randomized trials and integrative meta‑analyses. Collectively, these data confirm that psilocybin-assisted therapy produces moderate-to-large short‑term antidepressant effects in both major depressive disorder (MDD) and treatment‑resistant depression (TRD), with emerging evidence of durability out to 6–12 months and even up to five years in carefully selected samples.[5][19][25][28][41][50][22][71][23] At the same time, more rigorous trial designs and comparative analyses have tempered early enthusiasm, showing that when expectancy and blinding are handled more carefully, psilocybin and other psychedelic-assisted therapies are not clearly superior to traditional antidepressants in terms of average symptom reduction.[4][4][39][4][4][57][74] MDMA-assisted therapy has continued to demonstrate very large effects in post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) but remains in an early, safety‑feasibility phase for primary MDD, with encouraging open‑label data but no randomized MDD trials yet.[7][20][7][52][27][62] For ketamine, a growing contrast has emerged between earlier robust findings of rapid antidepressant effects and several recent negative or equivocal randomized trials using serial infusions or prolonged-release oral formulations in real‑world inpatient and TRD populations.[3][8][18][21][8][3][29] Meanwhile, esketamine nasal spray has consolidated its regulatory status with expanded approval and additional safety‑effectiveness data in clinical practice.[38][61][64] Overall, the evidence base now supports psychedelic‑assisted and ketamine-based therapies as credible antidepressant options with broadly acceptable safety profiles in controlled settings, but it also underscores that they are best understood as alternative or adjunctive approaches with comparable, rather than clearly superior, efficacy to modern antidepressants, and with important uncertainties regarding long‑term safety, scalability, and optimal use.[4][9][4][9][27][9][65][67][71][74]

## 1. Background: Depression, Treatment Gaps, and the Rise of Psychedelic-Assisted Therapies

Major depressive disorder and its treatment‑resistant forms remain among the leading causes of disability worldwide, and conventional antidepressant medications, while effective for many, leave a substantial proportion of patients with residual symptoms or nonresponse. Epidemiological and clinical data suggest that approximately half of individuals with MDD may meet criteria for treatment‑resistant depression when a stringent definition is applied, underscoring the need for novel mechanisms and therapeutic models.[4][9][9][71] Traditional monoaminergic antidepressants frequently require weeks to achieve full effect and often entail chronic daily dosing, with burdensome adverse effects such as sexual dysfunction, weight gain, emotional blunting, and withdrawal phenomena upon cessation.[4][4][39][4] These limitations have driven renewed interest in fast‑acting and experience‑modifying interventions capable of producing rapid and durable shifts in mood and functioning.

Within this context, psychedelic‑assisted therapy (PAT)—the combination of a high‑dose psychedelic experience with structured psychotherapeutic support—has re‑emerged as a promising paradigm. Classic psychedelics such as psilocybin and LSD, alongside entactogens like MDMA, were explored in psychiatry in the mid‑twentieth century, but regulatory restrictions curtailed research for decades.[27][27][65][67] Over the past fifteen years, a new wave of carefully controlled trials has demonstrated substantial symptom improvements across several conditions, particularly depression and PTSD, after only one to three dosing sessions combined with preparatory and integrative psychotherapy.[9][9][9][45][9][65] Ketamine, an N‑methyl‑D‑aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonist with rapid-acting antidepressant properties, has followed a somewhat parallel trajectory, progressing from off‑label IV use to the regulatory approval of intranasal esketamine for treatment‑resistant depression and acute suicidal ideation in MDD.[34][38][61][64][70][73]

By 2024–2025, this field reached a pivotal point. Psilocybin entered Phase 3 development for both TRD and MDD, with several large randomized trials reporting positive topline results and long‑term follow‑up data suggesting sustained benefit for some patients.[11][25][32][36][48][51][58][60][22][63][16][71] MDMA-assisted therapy achieved two successful Phase 3 trials in PTSD but encountered a major regulatory setback when the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) declined approval, citing methodological concerns and safety uncertainties.[2][12][17][52][58][62][65][69] Ketamine research, in turn, produced some of the most methodologically rigorous tests to date of serial infusions and prolonged‑release oral formulations, including several trials that failed to show clear superiority over active controls under stringent conditions.[3][8][18][21][8][3][29] At the same time, meta‑analyses began directly comparing psychedelic-assisted therapy with traditional antidepressants, reshaping the narrative around their relative efficacy.[4][4][4][4][57][67][74]

## 2. Clinical Development Landscape and Regulatory Context

Understanding the latest trial results requires situating them within the broader developmental and regulatory landscape. In the United States and Europe, regulators have increasingly engaged with psychedelic therapies through mechanisms such as *breakthrough therapy* designation and accelerated review pathways, even as they apply higher scrutiny to trial design, safety monitoring, and real‑world implementation models.[53][27][58][60][63][65][66] Psilocybin and MDMA, in particular, have received breakthrough designations for depression and PTSD, respectively, reflecting early evidence of substantial improvements over available therapies in serious, treatment‑resistant conditions.[53][27][60][62][65][66]

Psilocybin is furthest along in development for depression. Compass Pathways' synthetic formulation COMP360 completed two pivotal Phase 3 trials in TRD—COMP005 and COMP006—with both studies meeting their primary endpoints of greater symptom reduction on the Montgomery‑Åsberg Depression Rating Scale (MADRS) compared with low‑dose psilocybin or placebo.[11][16][16][48][51][58][63][16] In parallel, the non‑profit Usona Institute launched the uAspire Phase 3 trial of psilocybin 25 mg for MDD, following positive Phase 2 evidence (PSIL201) and with a design explicitly aimed at characterizing one‑year durability and long‑term safety.[32][36][60] Additional corporate programs, such as Cybin's deuterated psilocybin analogue CYB003, have released Phase 2 data in MDD and are now entering Phase 3 under the PARADIGM program, also with a focus on sustained effects beyond three to four months.[31][33][37][53]

MDMA's development has been most advanced in PTSD rather than depression. Phase 3 trials MAPP1 and MAPP2 showed large and clinically meaningful reductions in PTSD symptoms, with high response and remission rates, and contributed to the FDA's earlier decision to grant MDMA-assisted therapy breakthrough‑therapy status for PTSD.[12][17][27][17][52][27][62][66] However, in 2024 the FDA rejected Lykos Therapeutics' New Drug Application (NDA), after an advisory committee expressed concerns about risk of bias, unblinding, trial conduct, and the sufficiency of safety data, including potential cardiovascular and abuse-related risks.[2][12][58][65][69] This ruling has major implications for all psychedelic drug development, as it signals that even highly positive Phase 3 results may not be enough without robust methodological safeguards and comprehensive safety characterizations.

Ketamine and esketamine occupy a somewhat different position, as ketamine has long been an approved anesthetic and esketamine is already FDA-approved for TRD and depressive episodes with acute suicidal ideation or behavior.[34][38][61][64][70][73] In 2024, the FDA expanded Spravato (esketamine) approval to allow monotherapy for TRD, reflecting accumulated evidence from RCTs and real‑world studies showing significant symptom reductions and tolerable safety profiles when used under a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) program.[38][61][64] At the same time, several new investigational formulations entered clinical testing, including extended‑release oral ketamine (KET01) and extended‑release ketamine tablets (R‑107), but these have yielded mixed or statistically non‑significant efficacy signals to date.[8][21][8]

Beyond individual compounds, regulatory bodies have also taken systemic steps to prioritize treatments for serious mental illness. National priority voucher schemes have been introduced to accelerate development of psilocybin for TRD and MDD, as well as other novel agents like methylone for PTSD.[60][65] Epidemiologic modeling suggests that, pending approval, psilocybin-assisted therapy could be applicable to a very large segment of the U.S. depression population: analyses indicate that between 56% and 62% of individuals currently being treated for MDD and TRD—approximately 5.1 to 5.6 million people—would meet typical eligibility criteria used in psilocybin trials.[59]

## 3. Psilocybin for Depression: Efficacy, Durability, and Safety

### 3.1 Mechanisms and Rationale

Psilocybin is a prodrug converted in vivo to psilocin, which acts primarily as a potent agonist at the serotonin 5-HT2A receptor, with additional activity at other serotonergic receptors.[35][40][53][56][67] Preclinical and translational research suggests that activation of 5-HT2A receptors by psilocybin engages cortical networks that regulate cognition, emotion, and self‑referential processing, disrupting entrenched patterns of negative thinking and potentially facilitating cognitive and emotional flexibility.[35][40][53][56][67] Neurobiological models increasingly converge on enhanced neuroplasticity as a key mechanistic pathway: psilocybin and related psychedelics have been shown in animal and cellular models to promote dendritic remodeling, synaptogenesis, and neurogenesis, as well as to modulate inflammatory and stress‑related pathways.[35][40][53][56][67]

### 3.2 Randomized Trials in Major Depressive Disorder

The most definitive evidence for psilocybin in non‑treatment‑resistant MDD comes from randomized controlled trials using single or limited dosing combined with structured psychotherapy. A pivotal trial evaluated a single 25‑mg dose of psilocybin plus psychological support versus an active placebo (niacin) in adults with moderate-to-severe MDD.[28] Psilocybin produced a substantially greater reduction in MADRS scores from baseline to day 43 compared with niacin, with a mean between‑group difference of approximately 12 points.[28] No serious treatment‑emergent adverse events occurred, but psilocybin was associated with higher rates of overall and severe AEs, emphasizing that while serious toxicity was absent, the acute experience commonly involves challenging symptoms such as anxiety, nausea, headache, and transient psychological distress.[28]

A systematic review published in 2024 identified five RCTs including 472 adult patients with MDD: three of the five trials found psilocybin significantly more effective than the control condition, while two reported mixed results.[23][23] Meta‑analytic syntheses calculated standardized mean differences in depression scores favoring psilocybin with pooled effect sizes ranging from approximately 0.6 to 1.0 — moderate‑to‑large range.[5][19][9][9][22][23]

### 3.3 Phase 3 Trials in TRD (COMP005 & COMP006)

The COMP005 trial (258 participants, 32 U.S. sites) compared a single 25‑mg dose of COMP360 with placebo. Topline results showed a statistically significant reduction in MADRS scores at six weeks — mean treatment difference approximately −3.6 points (p < 0.001). Symptom improvements were evident from the day after administration and maintained through week 6, with durability data indicating continued benefit up to week 26.[11][16][48][51][63]

COMP006 (dose‑repetition design: two 25‑mg doses three weeks apart) also met its primary endpoint — mean MADRS difference of about −3.8 points (p < 0.001), 39% of patients achieving ≥25% MADRS reduction.[11][48][51][63] Safety: AEs predominantly mild to moderate, occurring mainly on dosing days, resolving within 24 hours; serious suicidal events <1% in 25‑mg arms.

### 3.4 Long-Term Outcomes

A long‑term observational follow‑up reported approximately 67% of MDD patients in remission at five years, compared with 58% at one year, with no severe treatment‑related adverse events.[41][47][50] In TRD, 75% maintained response and 58% achieved remission at one year after two psilocybin doses.[2][26]

### 3.5 CYB003 (Cybin's deuterated psilocybin analogue)

Phase 2 MDD trial: single 12‑mg dose produced a mean −14‑point MADRS difference at three weeks vs placebo. After two doses: response/remission rates ~79% at six weeks, ~75% in remission at four months. No drug‑related serious AEs, no suicidal ideation.[31][33][37]

### 3.6 Safety Summary

Psilocybin's safety profile: frequent but transient acute AEs (headache, nausea, anxiety, elevated BP), rare serious complications. HPPD documented in one TRD RCT case. Suicidal ideation occurred across all dose groups in TRD studies, driven primarily by baseline severity, not drug effect.[14][26][27][68][71]

## 4. MDMA-Assisted Therapy and Major Depressive Disorder

### 4.1 PTSD Phase 3 Evidence

MAPP1 and MAPP2 trials: mean CAPS‑5 reduction −24.4 vs −13.9 (MAPP1), −23.7 vs −14.8 (MAPP2). After three MDMA sessions, 67–71% no longer met PTSD criteria vs 32–48% placebo. FDA rejected Lykos NDA in 2024 — concerns about bias, unblinding, insufficient safety data.[2][12][58][65][69]

### 4.2 Open-Label MDD Trial

Single-arm study: MDMA‑AT in moderate-to-severe MDD (baseline MADRS ~29.6). Post-treatment MADRS reduction: −19.3 points (95% CI −23.8 to −14.8; p < 0.001). Sheehan Disability Scale reduction: −11.7 points. No serious adverse events. Safety and feasibility demonstrated — RCTs needed to confirm.[7][20]

### 4.3 Status for Depression

MDMA‑AT for depression = proof-of-concept stage only. No RCTs in primary MDD. No suicide attempts or completed suicides in any MDMA-AT trial. The FDA rejection for PTSD has raised the bar for methodological rigor for any future MDD application.

## 5. Ketamine and Esketamine for Depression

### 5.1 Historical Efficacy

Single IV infusion (0.5 mg/kg/40 min): response rates 50–70% within 24 hours in TRD; meta‑analyses show 77% response rate and 43% remission within 4–72 hours. Effects typically wane after 7–14 days.[13][34][42][44][70][73]

### 5.2 New RCTs — Mixed Results

**Inpatient serial infusions RCT:** Adjunctive ketamine vs midazolam, hospitalized patients. No significant difference in MADRS at end of treatment (adjusted mean difference −3.16 points, 95% CI −8.54 to 2.22; p = 0.25). No differences at 6-month follow-up either.[3][18][29]

**KET01 (oral prolonged-release, 240 mg/day):** Numerically greater MADRS reduction vs placebo at day 15 (17 vs 23 points, Δ~5 pts), but not statistically significant (p ≈ 0.15). Trial terminated early.[21]

**R-107 (extended-release ketamine tablets):** No dose reached statistical significance for primary endpoint at day 92 (best: 120 mg/day, mean difference 4.5 pts, 95% CI −0.60 to 9.69; p = 0.083).[8]

### 5.3 Esketamine — Expanded Approval

FDA expanded Spravato to monotherapy for TRD in 2024. Key supporting trial: remission at week 4 = 22.5% esketamine vs 7.6% placebo.[64] Safety: dissociation, dizziness, sedation, transient BP elevation — manageable, no new signals. REMS program retained.[38][61][64]

## 6. Comparative Analyses — Psychedelics vs Traditional Antidepressants

### 6.1 Meta-Analyses of Psilocybin

- *Frontiers in Psychiatry* 2024 (8 studies, 524 patients): Hedges' g = −0.89 (95% CI −1.25 to −0.53); response OR ~10.24.[5]
- Umbrella review: Hedges' g ≈ 1.05 (95% CI 0.84–1.26) for MDD, benefits to 6 months.[9]
- RCT-focused meta-analysis (6 RCTs, 427 patients): SMD at 1 week g = −0.72 (95% CI −0.95 to −0.49; I² = 17%); response RR ~3.4, remission RR ~3.7.[19]

### 6.2 JAMA Psychiatry 2026 — The Critical Finding

Williams, Barnett & Szigeti meta-analysis: compared psychedelic trials with open-label antidepressant trials (matching expectancy/unblinding levels). **Result: essentially no difference** — mean difference ~0.3 HAM-D units favoring antidepressants, clinically negligible.[4][57][74]

Both treatments produced ~12-point reductions on a 52-point scale. Neither clearly superior.

**Interpretation:** Earlier claims of psychedelic superiority were substantially driven by placebo suppression from functional unblinding (>90% of psychedelic participants correctly guess their allocation), not true pharmacologic advantage. Psychedelics are comparable to antidepressants in average efficacy — different in dosing schedule, subjective experience, and side-effect profile.[4][57][74]
